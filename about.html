  • 1887

    Early Life

    Srinivasa Ramanujan was born in Erode, Tamil Nadu, India.

  • 1890-1905

    Early Education and Self-Study

    Ramanujan showed an early aptitude for mathematics, exploring concepts on his own and developing his own theorems. Due to financial constraints, he faced challenges in pursuing formal education, but he continued to study mathematics independently.

  • 1910

    Discovery of Pi Formulas

    Ramanujan independently discovered formulas to calculate the mathematical constant pi (Ï€), which are now known as Ramanujan's pi formulas.

  • 1911

    Correspondence with Hardy

    Ramanujan's mathematical work came to the attention of the British mathematician G.H. Hardy through a letter with samples of his theorems. Hardy recognized Ramanujan's genius and invited him to England to further explore his mathematical ideas.

  • 1914

    Move to England

    Ramanujan moved to Cambridge, England, to collaborate with G.H. Hardy at the University of Cambridge. He faced challenges in adjusting to the English academic environment but continued to produce groundbreaking work

  • 1916

    Scholarships and papers published

    Ramanujan was awarded a Bachelor of Science degree by research (Ph.D. equivalent) for his work on highly composite numbers. He became a Fellow of the Royal Society, a prestigious honor for his contributions to mathematics.

  • 1919

    Health Challenges and Return to India

    Ramanujan's health deteriorated due to a combination of factors, including the climate and dietary changes in England. He returned to India in 1919 and continued his mathematical work, despite declining health.

  • 1920

    Death and Legacy

    January 26, 1920: Srinivasa Ramanujan passed away at the young age of 32 due to health complications. His notebooks contained a wealth of mathematical discoveries that continue to influence and inspire mathematicians to this day.
